Iron-gall
ink.

A dark, permanent writing ink. The classic recipe is older than the calendars; the chemistry is simple and the patience required is not.

An iron-gall ink is what you get when tannin meets iron in a slightly acidic bath. The result is initially pale and turns deep blue-black on the page as it oxidises — the page does most of the work, which is the point. We measure in handfuls and hours, not grams and seconds1.

Materials

  • Oak galls — a small handful, crushed. Wasp galls are best; cynips quercusfolii if you can name them.
  • Ferrous sulfate (FeSO₄) — about a teaspoon, dissolved in warm water.
  • Gum arabic — pea-sized lump, for body and to keep the iron in suspension.
  • Cloves or a drop of wintergreen — preservation. A small mercy against mould.
¶
Footnote

The medieval recipe asks for wine instead of water. Wine works; it also stains. Water is fine. Use what's nearest.

Method

  1. Steep the crushed galls in water for two to three days. Cover. Stir on memory of it.
  2. Strain the tannin liquor through cheesecloth. The colour should be a thin, pale tea — almost disappointing.
  3. Dissolve the ferrous sulfate separately. Add it to the tannin liquor in a slow ribbon, stirring. The mixture darkens immediately.
  4. Stir in the gum arabic. Bottle. Label.
# iron-gall ink — small batch
steep  galls      "a small handful, crushed"  72h
add    FeSO₄      "1 tsp in warm water"      1×
bind   gum arabic "pea-sized lump"            1×
keep   cloves     "three, whole"              ∞
!
Check

Too much iron and the ink will eventually eat through the paper. Old manuscripts complain about this; modern ones still do.

Variations

The classical recipe is a starting point. Substitute pomegranate rinds for galls and you get a softer brown2. Replace ferrous sulfate with copper sulfate for a green-black that ages oddly. Adding a single clove changes the smell, not the chemistry — but smell is part of the recipe.

On waiting

The ink looks wrong for the first hour. It will look right after a day. See also: walnut hull stain.

  1. 1. The exception is the iron, which you should weigh. The page is permanent; the binding is not.
  2. 2. A brown not unlike old photographs. Use on cream paper.
